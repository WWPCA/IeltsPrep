IELTS Speaking Parts

The examiner introduces themselves and asks you to introduce yourself and confirm your identity. They will then ask general questions on familiar topics such as:

  • Home
  • Family
  • Work or studies
  • Hobbies and interests
  • Daily routines

This part tests your ability to communicate opinions and information on everyday topics by answering a range of questions.

The examiner gives you a task card with a topic and some points to include in your talk. You have 1 minute to prepare and make notes, then you need to talk about the topic for 1-2 minutes. Topics are usually about:

  • Describing a person, place, object, or event
  • Talking about an experience or memory
  • Explaining a preference or decision

After your talk, the examiner may ask one or two brief questions on the same topic. This part tests your ability to speak at length with minimal hesitation and to use language accurately and appropriately.

The examiner asks further questions connected to the topic in Part 2, allowing a deeper discussion of more abstract ideas and issues. Questions typically ask you to:

  • Analyze situations or problems
  • Discuss hypothetical scenarios
  • Evaluate different options or viewpoints
  • Make comparisons or speculate about the future

This part tests your ability to explain and justify opinions and to analyze, discuss and speculate about issues.

Official IELTS Speaking Test Format and Structure

The IELTS examiner introduces themselves and asks you to introduce yourself and confirm your identity. They will then ask a series of questions on familiar topics such as:

  • Your home or accommodation
  • Your family and friends
  • Your work or studies
  • Your hobbies and interests
  • Your daily routines and activities
  • Your hometown or country

This part of the IELTS Speaking test assesses your ability to communicate opinions and information on everyday topics by answering a range of questions. You will be evaluated on your fluency, pronunciation, vocabulary use, and grammatical accuracy.

In IELTS Speaking Part 2, the examiner gives you a task card with a topic and specific points to include in your talk. You have exactly 1 minute to prepare and make notes, then you must speak about the topic for 1-2 minutes without interruption. Common IELTS Speaking Part 2 topics include:

  • Describing a person (friend, family member, teacher, etc.)
  • Describing a place (city, building, historical site, etc.)
  • Describing an object (something you own, gift, etc.)
  • Describing an event (celebration, festival, etc.)
  • Talking about a personal experience or memory
  • Explaining a preference, choice, or decision

After your long turn, the examiner may ask one or two brief follow-up questions on the same topic. This part evaluates your ability to speak at length with minimal hesitation and to organize your ideas coherently while using varied vocabulary and grammar structures appropriately.

In IELTS Speaking Part 3, the examiner asks further questions connected to the topic from Part 2, but these questions explore more abstract ideas and issues. This section is designed to test your critical thinking skills and ability to express complex thoughts in English. You may be asked to:

  • Analyze social situations or global problems
  • Discuss hypothetical scenarios and their implications
  • Evaluate different options, perspectives, or viewpoints
  • Make comparisons between different time periods or cultures
  • Speculate about future trends or developments
  • Express and justify your opinions on broader issues

This most challenging part of the IELTS Speaking test evaluates your ability to develop ideas thoroughly, use a wide range of vocabulary and complex sentence structures, maintain fluency while discussing abstract concepts, and demonstrate critical thinking ability in English. Your band score will reflect your performance across all four assessment criteria.
